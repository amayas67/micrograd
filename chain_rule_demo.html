⛓️ Chain Rule Proof

Deriving the multivariate chain rule from the composite derivative formula

📐 Prerequisite — The Composite Derivative Formula

We start from the composite derivative formula (single-variable chain rule). If $L$ is a function of $u$, and $u$ is a function of $x$, then:

$$\frac{dL}{dx} = \frac{dL}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}$$

This means that a small change $h$ in $x$ produces a change in $L$ of:

$$L\bigl(u(x+h)\bigr) - L\bigl(u(x)\bigr) = \frac{dL}{du} \cdot u'(x) \cdot h$$

🎯 Goal

Consider a composite function:

$$L = L\bigl(f(x),\; g(x)\bigr)$$

We want to compute the derivative of $L$ with respect to $x$, that is, $\dfrac{dL}{dx}$.

💡 Key idea: $L$ takes two arguments, so we can't apply the composite derivative formula directly. But if we fix one function at a time, the rule becomes applicable to each path separately.

1 Fix $g$ and apply the formula through $f$

We fix $g$ (i.e., $g(x)$ stays constant) and only let $f$ vary. Now $L\bigl(f(x),\, g(x)\bigr)$ is just $L$ composed with $f$ — the composite derivative formula applies directly.

When $x \to x + h$, $f$ changes by $f'(x) \cdot h$, so:

$$L\bigl(f(x+h),\; g(x)\bigr) = L\bigl(f(x),\; g(x)\bigr) + \frac{\partial L}{\partial f} \cdot f'(x) \cdot h$$
↳ This is the composite derivative formula applied to the $f$ path, with $g$ held fixed.

2 Now do the same with $g$

We now also let $g$ vary: $g(x) \to g(x+h)$. Applying the composite derivative formula through the $g$ path adds a new term. But notice carefully: we are varying $g$ starting from the state where $f$ has already moved to $f(x+h)$.

So the derivative with respect to $g$ is evaluated at the new point $(f(x+h), g(x))$:

$$L\bigl(f(x+h),\; g(x+h)\bigr) = L\bigl(f(x+h),\; g(x)\bigr) + \frac{\partial L}{\partial g}\bigg|_{(f(x+h),\, g(x))} \cdot g'(x) \cdot h$$

Substituting the result from Step 1:

$$L\bigl(f(x+h),\; g(x+h)\bigr) = L\bigl(f(x),\; g(x)\bigr) + \frac{\partial L}{\partial f} \cdot f'(x) \cdot h + \frac{\partial L}{\partial g}\bigg|_{(f(x+h),\, g(x))} \cdot g'(x) \cdot h$$
↳ Both paths contribute, but the $g$-path derivative is evaluated at the shifted point $f(x+h)$, not $f(x)$!

3 Subtract $L$ from both sides and divide by $h$

Subtracting $L\bigl(f(x), g(x)\bigr)$:

$$L\bigl(f(x+h),\; g(x+h)\bigr) - L\bigl(f(x),\; g(x)\bigr) = \frac{\partial L}{\partial f} \cdot f'(x) \cdot h + \frac{\partial L}{\partial g}\bigg|_{(f(x+h),\, g(x))} \cdot g'(x) \cdot h$$

Dividing by $h$:

$$\frac{L\bigl(f(x+h),\; g(x+h)\bigr) - L\bigl(f(x),\; g(x)\bigr)}{h} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial f} \cdot f'(x) + \frac{\partial L}{\partial g}\bigg|_{(f(x+h),\, g(x))} \cdot g'(x)$$

⚠️ Subtlety — The Shifted Evaluation Point

Notice the term $\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial g}\bigg|_{(f(x+h),\, g(x))}$. The partial derivative is evaluated at $f(x+h)$, not $f(x)$. Yet, the classical chain rule formula evaluates everything at $(f(x), g(x))$.

How do we justify replacing $f(x+h)$ with $f(x)$?

When we take the limit $h \to 0$:

  1. $f(x+h) \to f(x)$ by continuity of $f$ (every differentiable function is continuous)
  2. $\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial g}\bigg|_{(f(x+h),\, g(x))} \to \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial g}\bigg|_{(f(x),\, g(x))}$ by continuity of the partial derivative
$$\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\partial L}{\partial g}\bigg|_{(f(x+h),\, g(x))} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial g}\bigg|_{(f(x),\, g(x))}$$
🔑 Key requirement: This is why we need $L$ to be of class $C^1$ (continuously differentiable). Without continuity of the partial derivatives, this "sliding" of the evaluation point would not be justified!

✅ Conclusion — The Chain Rule

Taking the limit as $h \to 0$, the shifted evaluation point converges to $(f(x), g(x))$:

$$\boxed{\frac{dL}{dx} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial f}\bigg|_{(f(x),\, g(x))} \cdot \frac{df}{dx} + \frac{\partial L}{\partial g}\bigg|_{(f(x),\, g(x))} \cdot \frac{dg}{dx}}$$

The total derivative of $L$ is the sum of contributions from each path. Each contribution is obtained by fixing the other functions and applying the composite derivative formula. The continuity of partial derivatives ensures all terms can be evaluated at the same base point $(f(x), g(x))$.

🔗 Application in micro_grade.py

In the code, each operation (__add__, __mul__, tanh) defines a _backward() function that computes local gradients.

For example, for multiplication out = self * other:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \text{self}} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \text{out}} \cdot \text{other.data} \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial \text{other}} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \text{out}} \cdot \text{self.data}$$

This is exactly the chain rule: the local gradient $\dfrac{d(\text{out})}{d(\text{self})} = \text{other.data}$ is multiplied by the upstream gradient out.grad to obtain each node's gradient.

🔄 Propagation is done via reversed topological sort: we traverse the graph from output to input, accumulating gradients with +=.
