⛓️ Chain Rule Proof

Deriving the multivariate chain rule from the composite derivative formula

📐 Prerequisite — The Composite Derivative Formula

We start from the composite derivative formula (single-variable chain rule). If $L$ is a function of $u$, and $u$ is a function of $x$, then:

$$\frac{dL}{dx} = \frac{dL}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}$$

This means that a small change $h$ in $x$ produces a change in $L$ of:

$$L\bigl(u(x+h)\bigr) - L\bigl(u(x)\bigr) = \frac{dL}{du} \cdot u'(x) \cdot h$$

🎯 Goal

Consider a composite function:

$$L = L\bigl(f(x),\; g(x)\bigr)$$

We want to compute the derivative of $L$ with respect to $x$, that is, $\dfrac{dL}{dx}$.

💡 Key idea: $L$ takes two arguments, so we can't apply the composite derivative formula directly. But if we fix one function at a time, the rule becomes applicable to each path separately.

1 Fix $g$ and apply the formula through $f$

We fix $g$ (i.e., $g(x)$ stays constant) and only let $f$ vary. Now $L\bigl(f(x),\, g(x)\bigr)$ is just $L$ composed with $f$ — the composite derivative formula applies directly.

When $x \to x + h$, $f$ changes by $f'(x) \cdot h$, so:

$$L\bigl(f(x+h),\; g(x)\bigr) = L\bigl(f(x),\; g(x)\bigr) + \frac{\partial L}{\partial f} \cdot f'(x) \cdot h$$
↳ This is the composite derivative formula applied to the $f$ path, with $g$ held fixed.

2 Now do the same with $g$

We now also let $g$ vary: $g(x) \to g(x+h)$. Applying the composite derivative formula through the $g$ path adds a new term:

$$L\bigl(f(x+h),\; g(x+h)\bigr) = L\bigl(f(x),\; g(x)\bigr) + \frac{\partial L}{\partial f} \cdot f'(x) \cdot h + \frac{\partial L}{\partial g} \cdot g'(x) \cdot h$$
↳ Both paths contribute: the $f$ path and the $g$ path, each via the composite derivative formula.

3 Subtract $L$ from both sides and divide by $h$

Subtracting $L\bigl(f(x), g(x)\bigr)$:

$$L\bigl(f(x+h),\; g(x+h)\bigr) - L\bigl(f(x),\; g(x)\bigr) = \frac{\partial L}{\partial f} \cdot f'(x) \cdot h + \frac{\partial L}{\partial g} \cdot g'(x) \cdot h$$

Dividing by $h$:

$$\frac{L\bigl(f(x+h),\; g(x+h)\bigr) - L\bigl(f(x),\; g(x)\bigr)}{h} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial f} \cdot f'(x) + \frac{\partial L}{\partial g} \cdot g'(x)$$

✅ Conclusion — The Chain Rule

Taking the limit as $h \to 0$:

$$\boxed{\frac{dL}{dx} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial f} \cdot \frac{df}{dx} + \frac{\partial L}{\partial g} \cdot \frac{dg}{dx}}$$

The total derivative of $L$ is the sum of contributions from each path. Each contribution is obtained by fixing the other functions and applying the composite derivative formula.

🔗 Application in micro_grade.py

In the code, each operation (__add__, __mul__, tanh) defines a _backward() function that computes local gradients.

For example, for multiplication out = self * other:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \text{self}} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \text{out}} \cdot \text{other.data} \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial \text{other}} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \text{out}} \cdot \text{self.data}$$

This is exactly the chain rule: the local gradient $\dfrac{d(\text{out})}{d(\text{self})} = \text{other.data}$ is multiplied by the upstream gradient out.grad to obtain each node's gradient.

🔄 Propagation is done via reversed topological sort: we traverse the graph from output to input, accumulating gradients with +=.
